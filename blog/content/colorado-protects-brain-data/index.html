Colorado's State of Mind: Groundbreaking Neurological Data Protection

Posted on: Wed Apr 17 2024

In a historic first, the state of Colorado has taken a bold step towards the future of privacy rights in the digital era. A new law has been signed into action by Governor Jared Polis, extending privacy rights to a realm hitherto untouched: neural data.

The legislation, H.B. 24-1058, incorporates data generated by activity in the human nervous system into the personal information protected by Colorado's existing consumer privacy law. This marks a significant milestone in the evolution of privacy rights, acknowledging the increasing relevance of brain-computer interfaces and neurotechnology into mainstream society.

Companies such as Elon Muskâ€™s Neuralink are rapidly developing such brain-computer interfaces, with the stated goal of changing lives for the better through pioneering healthcare uses. The company envisions its devices being used to control prosthetics, restoring movement in individuals with paralysis or amputations. The technology potentially may open doors to human enhancement through memory augmentation and enhanced cognitive abilities.

On the downside, when our thoughts become digital data, they may be misused in the same ways "conventional" digital data has been. For example, exposure of highly sensitive personal information to the public, or even using the implanted devices to control individuals or influence their decision-making. The Colorado legislation sends a powerful message about the importance of proactive data protection, and reflects a deep understanding of the implications of rapidly advancing technology.

The law sets a precedent for other states, and in less than 24-hours since its passage, it is catalyzing a global conversation about the need for neuro-rights in an increasingly digital world. This move by Colorado not only expands the scope of what personal data protection means but also paves the way for a future where our most intimate data - in essence our thoughts - are safeguarded against misuse.

The implications of the law will undoubtedly be closely watched by privacy advocates, technology companies, lawmakers... and of course lawyers.
